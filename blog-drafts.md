## 1. Keep, donate, maybe
**Category:** Closet edit · 2 min

Every closet edit starts the same way: everything comes out, and we build three piles. Keep. Donate. Maybe.

Keep is the easy one. It's what you reach for without thinking — the jeans you'd grab if the house were on fire.

Donate is where people freeze. So I use one rule to break the tie: if it's ill-fitting, if it "sort of fits," or if the only reason it's still hanging there is *I might need this someday* — it goes.

That's the whole rule. "Sort of fits" is a no. "Someday" is a no. You are not a warehouse for a version of yourself that hasn't shown up in four years.

Maybe is the pile that makes the whole thing work. Anything you can't decide on goes there, and we don't argue about it — we move on. At the end, we come back and try to build an actual outfit out of each maybe. If it works with things you already own, it stays. If we can't style it, you have your answer, and you got there yourself instead of me telling you.

Here's what nobody warns you about: the hard part isn't the clothes, it's the story attached to them. The dress from a wedding. The jacket that cost too much to admit defeat on. The pants from before. I'm not attached to any of it, and that's most of what you're paying me for — an outside eye with no memory of what that sweater meant in 2019.

My clients routinely donate six to eight bags. One client and I filled eight. Nobody has ever called me later to say they miss something.

If you're doing this alone this weekend: pull everything out, don't sit down, and set a timer. The longer a piece sits in your hands, the more likely it goes back on the rack.

Three piles. One rule. Start.

## 2. Why getting dressed feels exhausting
**Category:** Wardrobe · 2 min

A closet full of clothes and nothing to wear is not a shopping problem. It's a decision problem.

Every morning you stand there and run the same calculation: what fits today, what's clean, what's appropriate, what matches, what did I wear last Tuesday when I saw these same people. That's a dozen decisions before coffee. Psychologists call it decision fatigue. I call it the reason you're late.

More clothes make it worse, not better. Every extra option is another thing to rule out. This is why a smaller, sharper wardrobe feels luxurious instead of limiting — you're not giving anything up, you're deleting the choices that were never real choices.

This is the actual case for a capsule wardrobe, and it has nothing to do with minimalism as an aesthetic. I don't care if your closet is beige. I care that when you open it, everything in there fits you, suits your life, and works with three other things next to it. Then getting dressed stops being a decision and becomes a reflex.

One client told me picking outfits used to be the worst part of her morning — indecisive, three changes before leaving the house. After we edited, her mornings got quick and easy and she actually felt confident in what she was wearing. Nothing about her taste changed. We just removed the noise.

Try this before you buy anything: for two weeks, turn every hanger backwards. When you wear something, hang it back the normal way. In fourteen days you'll see exactly what you actually wear — and I promise it's a much smaller set than what you own.

That's your real wardrobe. Everything else is just standing between you and the door.

## 3. Shop your closet before you shop anywhere else
**Category:** Shopping · 2 min

The best styling session I ever had with a client involved buying nothing.

Before I shop for anyone, we shop what's already hanging there. It's the fastest, cheapest win available, and it's where I hear the same sentence in almost every closet: *I would have never thought to put this together.* That's not magic. It's just that you've seen that blouse on that hanger next to that same cardigan for three years, and your brain filed it under "work shirt" and closed the folder.

Only after we've pulled every real outfit out of what you own do I look for gaps — and a gap is not "I want something new." A gap is a hole that's blocking three outfits from existing.

The pieces that close the most gaps are boring, and I mean that as a compliment:

- **A high-quality white t-shirt.** Not a three-pack. One that holds its shape and isn't see-through.
- **A perfect black blazer.** It has to fit your shoulders. Everything else can be tailored; shoulders can't.
- **A versatile sneaker.** One that works with denim and with a dress, so you stop buying a shoe per outfit.

Notice none of those are trends. Trends are fine — they're seasoning. These are the meal.

Buy in that order and something quiet happens: you shop less. One client told me she now wears everything in her closet and doesn't shop as much, because she finally knows what she has. That's the goal. I'm not trying to give you more clothes. I'm trying to make you stop panic-buying at 11pm because you have "nothing" for Saturday.

Shop your closet first. Fill the gaps second. Buy the trend third, if at all.

## 4. One suitcase, every day handled
**Category:** Packing · 2 min

More than half my work is trips. London, Mexico, a safari, back-to-back business travel. And the pattern is always the same: people don't overpack because they have too many clothes. They overpack because they haven't decided anything yet.

A suitcase is just a closet with a hard deadline. Every "just in case" item you throw in is a decision you're refusing to make, and you're paying for it later — in weight, in wrinkles, in standing over an open bag in a hotel room with nothing to wear.

Here's how I pack a client:

**Start with the calendar, not the clothes.** Write out the actual days. Dinner Thursday. Walking all day Friday. One nice night. That list is your packing list — not the weather app, not Pinterest.

**Pick three or four words.** With one client heading to London it was *effortless, crisp, layers*. Those words kill arguments at the suitcase. A piece either sounds like the words or it doesn't come.

**Everything must work with everything.** If a top only goes with one bottom, it's not earning its space. One palette, shoes that repeat, one thing that feels like an event.

**Build the outfits before you zip the bag.** Whole looks — shoes, jacket, jewelry — not a pile of components you'll assemble at 7am in a time zone you don't live in.

The point isn't a smaller bag. It's that you get to be *present* on your trip instead of negotiating with your luggage. One client said it best: she was able to be present on her trip and not stress about what she was wearing. Another said it's one less thing to worry about.

That's the whole product. Not outfits — attention. Pack so you can stop thinking about it.

## 5. Going to the mall shouldn't feel worse than the dentist
**Category:** Men's styling · 2 min

A male client once told me that shopping for clothes was, and I quote, "normally less fun than going to the dentist."

I hear a version of this from almost every guy I work with. Not that they don't care how they look — they care a lot. They just have no interest in spending a Saturday finding out. So they buy the same shirt in four colors, wear it until it dies, and repeat.

Here's the thing: men's styling isn't a different skill. It's the same three questions.

**Does it actually fit?** Not "is it my size." Sizes lie. Shoulders should end where your shoulders end. Sleeves break at the wrist. Everything else a tailor can fix for less than the price of the mistake you're about to make.

**Is it the brand or is it the fit?** This is the one that surprises people. Guys assume the label and the price tag make the look. They don't. Fit, plus complementary textures and colors, beats an expensive shirt hanging wrong — every time.

**Do you have too many options?** The men I work with are rarely short on clothes. They're drowning in them. One client went from overstuffed closets to a minimalist setup without compromising his style at all. His words afterward: he doesn't even remember what we got rid of.

So we go through everything, honestly. Some of it no longer fits. Some of it is a decade out of date. That's not an insult — it's just time passing. Then I build outfits from what survives and give you a short, specific list of what to buy, so the dreaded trip to the mall takes twenty minutes and you know exactly what you're looking for.

You don't have to enjoy shopping. You just have to stop dreading your closet.
