Packing · 2 min read

One suitcase, every day handled

More than half my work is trips. London, Mexico, a safari, back-to-back business travel. The pattern is always the same.

People don't overpack because they have too many clothes. They overpack because they haven't decided anything yet.

A suitcase is just a closet with a hard deadline. Every "just in case" item you throw in is a decision you're refusing to make, and you pay for it later — in weight, in wrinkles, in standing over an open bag in a hotel room with nothing to wear.

How I pack a client

Start with the calendar, not the clothes. Write out the actual days. Dinner Thursday. Walking all day Friday. One nice night. That list is your packing list — not the weather app, not Pinterest.

Pick three or four words. With one client heading to London it was effortless, crisp, layers. Those words end arguments at the suitcase. A piece either sounds like the words or it doesn't come.

Everything must work with everything. If a top only goes with one bottom, it isn't earning its space. One palette, shoes that repeat, one thing that feels like an event.

Build the outfits before you zip the bag. Whole looks — shoes, jacket, jewelry — not a pile of components you'll assemble at 7am in a time zone you don't live in.

The point isn't a smaller bag

It's that you get to be present on your trip instead of negotiating with your luggage. One client put it best: she was able to be present on her trip and not stress about what she was wearing. Another said it's simply one less thing to worry about.

That's the whole product. Not outfits — attention. Pack so you can stop thinking about it.

Your disheveled closet is my happy place.
