Why getting dressed feels exhausting

A closet full of clothes and nothing to wear is not a shopping problem. It's a decision problem.

Every morning you stand there and run the same calculation: what fits today, what's clean, what's appropriate, what matches, what did I wear last Tuesday when I saw these same people. That's a dozen decisions before coffee. Psychologists call it decision fatigue. I call it the reason you're late.

More clothes make it worse, not better. Every extra option is another thing to rule out.

This is the actual case for a capsule

A smaller, sharper wardrobe feels luxurious instead of limiting, because you're not giving anything up — you're deleting the choices that were never real choices.

And this has nothing to do with minimalism as an aesthetic. I don't care if your closet is beige. I care that when you open it, everything in there fits you, suits your life, and works with three other things next to it. Then getting dressed stops being a decision and becomes a reflex.

One client told me picking outfits used to be the worst part of her morning — indecisive, three changes before leaving the house. After we edited, her mornings got quick and easy, and she actually felt confident in what she was wearing. Nothing about her taste changed. We just removed the noise.

Try this before you buy anything

For two weeks, turn every hanger backwards. When you wear something, hang it back the normal way. In fourteen days you'll see exactly what you actually wear — and I promise it's a much smaller set than what you own.

That's your real wardrobe. Everything else is just standing between you and the door.

Your disheveled closet is my happy place.
